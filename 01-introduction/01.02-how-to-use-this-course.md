# How to Use This Course

This course is a series of **chapters**, each covering one feature area of EduFlow. You work through them in order, one at a time. The order is not arbitrary — every chapter builds on the one before it, so skipping ahead means standing on unstable ground.

## What each chapter does

Each chapter first **teaches** the concept. It explains the decision being made, compares the real alternatives, shows why the wrong approach is wrong (with a concrete cost — a query count, a response size, a leak), and explains why the right one wins. Then it **guides you to build** — naming the exact files and folders to create, the tables to add, the steps to take in order, and how to verify that each step worked. The teaching and the building happen side by side; you never have to finish a chapter to "go build something". You're building throughout.

Reading is woven through each chapter at the moment it deepens the work. When a concept arrives that has a strong external resource, the chapter points you to it right there — not at the start, not at the end, but when your curiosity is at its peak.

## The checklist is a gate

Every chapter ends in one of two things:

- A **Definition of Done** — a short list of observable outcomes you can see or demo. For example: "A `POST /api/auth/register` with a duplicate email returns 409, not 500."
- **Key Takeaways** — for concept chapters with nothing to build, a list of things you can now explain.

**The checklist is not a summary. It is the gate to the next chapter.** You do not move on until every box is ticked. This keeps the course honest in a self-paced setting: progress is earned, not skimmed. If three boxes are ticked and one is not, that unfinished box is where today's work is.

Close every chapter by asking yourself: can I demo this, or explain it to a colleague? If yes, tick and continue. If not, re-read the section that covers the missing item.

## Your learning log

You keep a **`learning-log/`** folder in your repo, with one file per chapter — for example, `learning-log/03-authentication.md`. Whenever a chapter asks "explain why…" or poses a reasoning question, you write the answer there. This is **mandatory**. The written answers are two things at once: evidence that you actually understood the chapter, and rehearsal for the final viva where you will be asked to explain your decisions out loud.

Commit the learning log as you go. A mentor or AI reviewer will read it. Don't write what you think sounds good — write what you actually understood, and note the parts that are still blurry.

## How you are judged

You are not graded on a quiz. You are graded on **understanding**, assessed at a final viva — a short live conversation where you walk through how your system works and answer "what would break if…" questions. The per-chapter checklists and your learning log are what keep you on track for that conversation.

You may use AI tools while building. The viva checks that you can explain your own code — not that you wrote every character.

## When you are stuck

In order:

1. Read the error message fully, not just the first line.
2. Read the docs for the specific function or library that's failing.
3. Write a smaller, isolated test to reproduce the problem.
4. Then ask — and when you ask, lead with "here's what I expected vs what happened."

Skipping to step 4 is the slowest path through a bug.
